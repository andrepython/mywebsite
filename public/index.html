Welcome

I am a Swiss academic researcher working at the intersection of statistics, geography, and public health. Currently based at the Center of Data Science, Zhejiang University (China), I investigate spatial patterns associated with natural and social phenomena.

My work focuses on phenomena that affect large communities globally — from infectious diseases to armed conflict. I believe in the power of evidence-based analysis to inform decision makers and policy.

If you are passionate about spatial data or want to discuss a problem you encounter in spatial analysis, feel free to contact me.

Obesity in Children

Childhood obesity is a growing global health concern with strong spatial heterogeneity. This project applies Bayesian spatial models to understand where risk is highest and what drives the geographic patterns.

Using large-scale survey data, we model the prevalence of overweight and obesity among children under five, capturing fine-scale geographic variation and its association with socio-economic, environmental, and demographic factors.

AI & Chinese Calligraphy

An interdisciplinary project applying deep learning and spatial analysis to the study of Shufa — traditional Chinese calligraphy.

Chinese calligraphy (書法, Shufa) carries rich cultural and aesthetic information embedded in stroke patterns, ink density, and spatial structure. We use convolutional neural networks to classify calligraphic styles, study master-student transmission of style, and model the geographic diffusion of distinct schools through history.

Methodology

CNN-based style classification combined with geo-referenced biographical data on historical calligraphers. Spatial analysis reveals how stylistic influence flowed across China's historical landscape.

About Me

I am a ZJU100 Young Professor at the Center of Data Science, Zhejiang University.

My first degree was in Geography at the University of Fribourg, Switzerland. In 2017, I completed a PhD in Statistics at the University of St Andrews (UK), developing a Bayesian spatio-temporal geostatistical model to capture fine-scale patterns of non-state terrorism across the world.

From 2018 to 2020, I worked as a postdoctoral research scientist in geospatial epidemiology at the Malaria Atlas Project, Big Data Institute, University of Oxford.

My current research interests are in applying statistical models to assess in space and time the risk of global threats such as contagious diseases or armed conflict. In line with the objectives of the Royal Statistical Society, I believe that statistics and data play a key role in society.
